    Method

    HOW THIS BRIEFING IS BUILT

    Open source. Reproducible. Updated weekly.

    The 19 risks

    Each one was picked for three reasons: we can measure it from public data, academic research keeps showing it really matters when systems fall apart, and it actually affects ordinary people's pay, rent and health. Climate, supply chain, food security and sovereign debt were added on 2026-04-25 to close the loop between geopolitics, economics and social pressure.

    Risk scores (0 to 1)

    Each risk has a score from 0 (calm) to 1 (crisis). 0.50 is the typical reading over the last decade. 0.70 or above is amber (worth watching). 0.85 or above is red (active problem). Scores are set each week by reading the main source for each risk and judging where it sits relative to its history.

    The cascade diagram

    The 54 arrows show how one risk can trigger another, based on academic research. The thickness of an arrow reflects how strong that link is. Arrows light up when one of their two ends is a top mover this week. When a number is built from several sources, you can see each source by clicking the circle.

    What changed this week

    Each week, the 5 risks that changed the most (up or down) are tagged "active" and rendered brighter on the diagram. The Now / Then / Next strip lists every risk that moved by 0.02 or more.

    Personal risk score

    Your overall score averages eight parts of your life (income, housing, health and so on). Each part gets adjusted up or down by what is happening in the world that week. The change number compares this week's starting score with last week's starting score; it ignores any safety nets you have switched on, so the comparison stays apples to apples.

    Sources and honesty

    Every card links to its main source. Each source is rated T1 (most reliable, official government statistics) through T5 (least reliable, sites that just collect numbers from other sites). When we show what comes next, we are drawing a straight line forward from the current trend, not running a clever model. The "confidence" label tells you how good our sources are, not the odds the prediction is right.
